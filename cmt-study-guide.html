Level I
Dow Theory
Charles Dow's foundational work, published in Wall Street Journal editorials from 1900–1902, remains the bedrock of technical analysis. Refined by William Hamilton and Robert Rhea.
Six Core Tenets
1. Averages discount everythingMarket prices reflect all known information — fundamentals, expectations, psychology. Nothing can remain hidden from the market for long. This is the EMH parallel in TA.
2. Three simultaneous trendsPrimary (months–years), secondary (weeks–months), and minor (days–weeks). Like tides, waves, and ripples on the ocean.
3. Three phases of primary trendsBull: Accumulation → Public Participation → Distribution. Bear: Distribution → Public Participation → Panic/Capitulation.
4. Averages must confirmOriginally DJIA + DJTA. A new high or low in one average must be confirmed by the other. Non-confirmation is a warning, not a signal on its own.
5. Volume confirms the trendVolume should expand in the direction of the primary trend and contract on corrections. Divergence between price and volume warns of weakening trend.
6. Trend persists until clear reversalThe benefit of the doubt goes to the prevailing trend. Ambiguous action or isolated reversals are not sufficient to call a trend change.
Three Phases of a Bull Market
Phase 1 — Accumulation
Smart money — institutional investors and insiders — buy depressed assets while public sentiment remains bearish. Price quietly builds a base. Volume is low. News is still negative.
Phase 2 — Public Participation
Improving fundamentals attract trend-following traders. Price trends strongly upward with increasing volume. This is the longest and most profitable phase. Business conditions improve visibly.
Phase 3 — Distribution
Public euphoria peaks. Smart money distributes holdings to latecomers. Volume is high but price advances slow. Divergences appear. Speculative excess is visible. Reversal is imminent.
Key Concepts
Dow vs. Modern TA

Dow Theory predates modern indicators but remains valid. The concept of confirmation has expanded — analysts now confirm across timeframes, asset classes, and multiple indicators rather than just two averages.

The three-phase framework maps closely to market cycle analysis and sector rotation models used in portfolio management.

Common Exam Traps

Non-confirmation ≠ reversal signal. It is a warning that requires follow-through.

Minor trends are manipulable according to Dow — do not trade minor trends alone.

Secondary trends retrace 33–67% of the prior primary move.

Level I
Chart Types & Construction
The method of displaying price data affects what signals are visible. Each chart type has specific strengths and use cases.
Chart Types Comparison
Line ChartPlots closing prices only. Cleanest view of overall trend. Loses intraday information. Best for long-term trend identification and clean visual analysis.
Bar Chart (OHLC)Open, High, Low, Close. Open = left tick, Close = right tick. The standard chart for Western technical analysis. Shows full price range and direction in each period.
Candlestick ChartSame data as bar chart but with a colour-coded body (real body). Bullish = close above open, Bearish = close below open. Faster to read visually, reveals sentiment.
Point & Figure (P&F)Time-independent. Records price movement only — X for rises, O for falls. Eliminates noise, shows pure supply/demand. Uses a box size and reversal amount.
Renko ChartTime-independent. Brick-based chart using fixed price moves. Smooths out minor fluctuations; shows trend clearly. Named from Japanese word for brick (renga).
Kagi ChartJapanese. Vertical lines reverse direction only after a defined reversal amount. Thick lines = price above prior high (yang); thin lines = below prior low (yin).
Chart Scales
Arithmetic (Linear)Equal vertical distance = equal price change. Suitable for short-term and range-bound analysis.
Logarithmic (Semi-log)Equal vertical distance = equal percentage change. Preferred for long-term charts and high-volatility assets. Shows growth in proportion.
Key Rule
Use log scale when the price range spans more than 2:1 ratio. Arithmetic scales distort percentage moves at high price levels.
Timeframes

Tick charts — every transaction. For scalping and microstructure analysis.

Intraday (1m–4h) — for day traders and short-term swing traders. Most noise; signals require confirmation.

Daily — primary timeframe for most technical analysis. Best balance of signal and noise.

Weekly/Monthly — identifies primary trend. Confirms or contradicts shorter-term signals.

Multiple timeframe analysis — top-down approach: identify trend on higher timeframe, time entries on lower timeframe.

Level I
Trends, Support, Resistance & Breakouts
The core principle of technical analysis — markets move in trends. Identifying, defining, and trading within trends is the primary skill of a technical analyst.
Trend Definitions
Uptrend
Series of Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). Each rally exceeds the prior rally peak; each pullback holds above the prior pullback low. Broken when price makes a Lower Low.
Downtrend
Series of Lower Highs (LH) and Lower Lows (LL). Each rally fails below the prior rally high; each selloff reaches a new low. Broken when price makes a Higher High.
Sideways / Ranging
Price oscillates between established support and resistance with no net directional movement. Often precedes a significant directional move.
Support & Resistance
SupportA price level where buying interest is sufficient to halt or reverse a decline. Demand zone. The more times a level is tested without breaking, the stronger it becomes.
ResistanceA price level where selling pressure is sufficient to halt or reverse an advance. Supply zone. Previous highs, round numbers, and prior S&R levels all create resistance.
Role ReversalOnce a support level is broken, it typically becomes resistance on any subsequent rally (and vice versa). This is the polarity principle — one of the most reliable phenomena in TA.
Round NumbersPsychological support/resistance at whole numbers (e.g., $100, $500, $1000). Reinforced by option strike clustering at round strikes.
Trendlines & Channels
Drawing Valid Trendlines

Uptrend line: connect two or more swing lows. Third touch confirms validity.

Downtrend line: connect two or more swing highs. Third touch confirms.

Steeper trendlines (>45°) are more fragile. A 45° trendline is considered ideal and sustainable.

A close beyond a trendline is more significant than an intraday pierce. Confirm with volume and follow-through.

Fan lines: three successively flatter trendlines after a major reversal signal trend exhaustion.

Price Channels

A channel draws a parallel line to the main trendline on the opposite side of price. Creates a dynamic price envelope.

Failure to reach the upper channel line in an uptrend is a warning of weakness. A break of the lower channel line signals trend reversal.

Channels can be used for target setting — projects the expected range of the next swing.

Fibonacci Retracements
Key Fibonacci Levels
Derived from the Golden Ratio (1.618). Applied to a prior swing move to identify potential reversal zones on the pullback.
23.6%Shallow. Common in strong impulsive moves where pullbacks are brief. Barely noticeable on a chart.
38.2%Moderate retracement. Often seen in strong trends. Typical Wave 4 target in Elliott Wave.
50.0%Not a Fibonacci ratio, but widely watched. The midpoint of any move. Psychologically significant.
61.8% ★The Golden Ratio retracement. Most significant level. Typical Wave 2 target. Strong potential reversal zone.
78.6%Square root of 61.8%. Deep retracement. A move to this level questions trend continuation but is not yet a reversal.
Retracement Level = High − (High − Low) × Fibonacci Ratio
Extension Level = Low + (High − Low) × Fibonacci Extension Ratio Common extensions: 127.2%, 161.8%, 261.8%
Breakouts & Retracements
Valid Breakout Criteria

Price close — closing beyond S/R is more significant than an intraday pierce (which can be a trap).

Volume confirmation — a breakout should occur on above-average volume. Volume on the breakout should exceed the average of the consolidation volume.

Follow-through — a second day of continuation beyond the breakout level reduces false breakout risk.

Pullback to S/R — a retest of the broken level that holds (role reversal) provides a high-probability secondary entry.

Stops & Risk

Structural stops — placed just beyond the key S/R or swing point that invalidates the trade setup.

ATR-based stops — N × ATR below entry (long) or above (short). Adjusts for current volatility regime.

Time stops — exit if price doesn't move in the expected direction within a defined timeframe. Useful for consolidation breakout trades.

Trailing stops — moves with price to lock in profits. Can use swing lows, moving averages, or ATR multiples.

Level I
Moving Averages
Moving averages smooth price data to identify trend direction, dynamic support/resistance, and generate entry/exit signals via crossovers.
Types of Moving Averages
Simple (SMA)Arithmetic mean of closing prices over N periods. Equal weight to all data points. Slower and smoother. Better for identifying key S/R levels.
Exponential (EMA)Applies exponentially greater weight to recent prices. More responsive to recent price action. Preferred for dynamic S/R and crossover signals.
Weighted (WMA)Linear weighting — most recent period gets the highest weight. Less common but useful for short-term responsiveness.
Hull (HMA)Uses weighted MAs and a square root period to minimise lag almost entirely. Reactive but can generate more false signals.
EMA = Price × k + EMA(prev) × (1 − k) where k = 2 / (N + 1) SMA = Sum(Close, N) / N
Moving Average Interactive Demo
Fast period 9
Slow period 21
Key Crossover Signals
Golden Cross50 SMA crosses above 200 SMA. Long-term bullish signal widely watched by institutions.
Death Cross50 SMA crosses below 200 SMA. Long-term bearish signal.
8/21 EMAIntermediate-term signal. Popular among CMT practitioners for trend identification.
20 EMA retestCommon intraday technique — pullback to 20 EMA on 5-min chart for continuation entry.
Bollinger Bands

Developed by John Bollinger. A moving average envelope using standard deviation to dynamically adjust band width to volatility.

Upper = MA(20) + 2σ Lower = MA(20) − 2σ Width = Upper − Lower (Bandwidth)

Squeeze — bands narrow to historically tight levels. Signals low volatility preceding a breakout move.

Walking the band — price consistently touches the upper or lower band in a strong trend.

Level I
Short-Term Patterns & Gaps
Short-term price patterns and gap analysis reveal near-term supply/demand imbalances. Most reliable when aligned with the prevailing trend direction.
Gap Types
Common GapOccurs in low-volume areas or trading ranges. Quickly filled. Little analytical significance. Also called area gap.
Breakaway GapOccurs on high volume as price breaks out of a consolidation pattern. Signals strong momentum. Often not filled for a long time.
Runaway Gap (Measuring Gap)Occurs midway through a trend move on increased volume. Signals trend continuation. Can be used to project the end of the move.
Exhaustion GapOccurs near the end of a trend on high volume. Soon followed by a reversal. Price quickly fills the gap. Often identified in hindsight.
Island ReversalExhaustion gap + reversal day + breakaway gap in the opposite direction. Strong reversal signal. Isolated price "island" on the chart.
Measuring Gap Target = Low of gap + (Low of gap − Start of move)
Key Short-Term Patterns
One-Day ReversalNew extreme (high/low) followed by a close in the opposite direction on high volume. Exhaustion signal.
Key Reversal DayBar makes new high (low) then closes below (above) prior close. More significant than a one-day reversal.
Inside DayHigh and low entirely within prior bar's range. Consolidation/indecision. Breakout from inside day can be significant.
Outside DayHigh and low both exceed prior bar's range. Volatility expansion. Direction determined by close.
Pipe Top/BottomTwo adjacent bars with nearly identical highs (tops) or lows (bottoms) and long wicks. Reversal pattern.
Price Targets & Measuring Rules

Head & Shoulders target: Neckline − (Head − Neckline)

Triangle target: Breakout ± base width of triangle at widest point

Flag/Pennant target: Breakout + length of the pole preceding pattern

Rectangle target: Breakout ± height of rectangle

Cup & Handle target: Breakout + depth of cup

These are minimum targets. In strong trends, price often significantly exceeds the measured move.

Level I
Volume Analysis
Volume is the fuel behind price moves. It validates price action and often leads price at key turning points. A move without volume is suspect.
Core Principles

Volume confirms trend: In an uptrend, volume should be heaviest on up days and lightest on down days. Divergence warns of weakening trend.

Breakout volume: A valid breakout from a pattern or S/R should occur on volume above the N-day average.

Climax volume: Extremely high volume (blowoff top or selling climax) often marks exhaustion and imminent reversal.

Volume precedes price: Changes in volume often anticipate price direction changes.

Volume Indicators
OBVOn Balance Volume. Cumulative volume — add on up days, subtract on down days. Trend should confirm price.
CMFChaikin Money Flow. Measures buying/selling pressure over a period using the close's location within the bar's range.
Volume Rate of ChangeMeasures the rate at which volume is changing. Spikes identify high-activity events.
Volume-Weighted MAMoving average giving more weight to prices traded at higher volume. More "true" than a simple MA.
Volume Profile Concepts
Point of Control (POC)The price level with the highest volume traded over a period. Strong magnet for price; key S/R reference.
Value Area (VA)The range of prices where approximately 70% of volume was traded. Breakouts from the value area often see rapid acceleration.
High Volume Node (HVN)Price area with heavy historical volume. Price tends to consolidate and slow at these zones.
Low Volume Node (LVN)Price area with minimal historical volume. Price tends to pass quickly through LVNs — targets the next HVN.
VWAPVolume-Weighted Average Price. Intraday benchmark. Institutional traders use it as a reference for execution quality. Acts as dynamic S/R.
OBV = OBV(prev) + Volume (if Close > Close(prev)) OBV = OBV(prev) − Volume (if Close < Close(prev)) CMF = Sum(Money Flow Volume, N) / Sum(Volume, N) Money Flow Multiplier = [(Close − Low) − (High − Close)] / (High − Low)
Level I
Candlestick Charting
Developed in Japan by Munehisa Homma in the 18th century for rice trading. Introduced to the West by Steve Nison. Candlesticks reveal market sentiment within each period.
Anatomy of a Candlestick
Single Candle Patterns
MarubozuNo wicks. Full session dominance by bulls (bullish) or bears (bearish). Strong continuation signal.
DojiOpen ≈ Close. Indecision. Significance depends on context and prior trend. More meaningful after extended moves.
Dragonfly DojiOpen = High = Close; long lower shadow. Bullish — sellers drove price down but buyers recovered all losses.
Gravestone DojiOpen = Low = Close; long upper shadow. Bearish — buyers drove price up but sellers pushed it all the way back.
HammerSmall body, long lower shadow (≥2× body), little/no upper shadow. Bullish after downtrend. Hanging Man is the same shape in an uptrend (bearish).
Shooting StarSmall body, long upper shadow (≥2× body), little/no lower shadow. Bearish after uptrend. Inverted Hammer is the same shape in a downtrend (bullish potential).
Spinning TopSmall body, upper and lower shadows. More balanced than a doji but still signals indecision.
Multi-Candle Patterns
Bullish Patterns
Bullish EngulfingLarge bullish candle completely engulfs prior bearish candle's body. Strong reversal signal after downtrend.
Piercing PatternBearish candle followed by bullish candle that opens below prior low and closes above midpoint. Bullish reversal.
Morning Star3-candle: large bearish → small body (gap) → large bullish. Classic bottom reversal pattern.
Three White SoldiersThree consecutive large bullish candles with each opening within prior body and closing at highs. Strong bullish continuation.
Bullish HaramiLarge bearish candle followed by small bullish candle contained within the first. Weaker reversal signal — requires confirmation.
Bearish Patterns
Bearish EngulfingLarge bearish candle completely engulfs prior bullish candle's body. Strong reversal after uptrend.
Dark Cloud CoverBullish candle followed by bearish candle that opens above prior high and closes below midpoint. Bearish reversal.
Evening Star3-candle: large bullish → small body (gap) → large bearish. Classic top reversal pattern.
Three Black CrowsThree consecutive large bearish candles with each opening within prior body and closing at lows. Strong bearish signal.
Bearish HaramiLarge bullish candle followed by small bearish candle contained within the first. Requires confirmation.
CMT Exam Focus
Context is critical — a hammer at a support level or after a Fibonacci retracement is far more significant than one appearing in the middle of a range. Confirmed patterns (next candle validates) carry more weight than unconfirmed. Abandoned baby (gap + doji + gap) is a particularly strong reversal signal tested on Level II.
Level I
Point & Figure Charting
A time-independent method that filters noise and focuses purely on significant price movements. Developed in the late 19th century by Charles Dow and refined by Thomas Dorsey.
Construction Rules

Box size: The minimum price increment required to add a new X or O. Commonly set as ATR or a fixed value. Determines sensitivity.

Reversal amount: The number of boxes in the opposite direction required to reverse from X column to O column (or vice versa). Typically 3 boxes.

X columns represent rising prices (demand). O columns represent falling prices (supply).

Time is not shown. Only price movement matters — a chart could represent one day or one year.

P&F Patterns & Signals
Double Top BuyX column exceeds prior X column high. Basic buy signal.
Double Bottom SellO column falls below prior O column low. Basic sell signal.
Triple Top BuyX column breaks above two prior X column highs. Stronger buy signal.
Bullish CatapultTriple top buy followed by pullback and renewed breakout. Very strong buy signal.
45° TrendlineUpward (bullish support) or downward (bearish resistance) lines drawn at 45° on the P&F grid.
Count Methods for Targets
Vertical Count (Bullish)Count the number of Xs in the first X column of the breakout × box size × reversal amount. Add to the low of the base.
Horizontal Count (Bullish)Count the number of columns in the base formation × box size × reversal amount. Add to the base.
Bullish Vertical Target = Low + (# of Xs in column × Box Size × Reversal Amount) Bearish Vertical Target = High − (# of Os in column × Box Size × Reversal Amount)
Level I
Confirmation
No single indicator or signal should be acted on in isolation. Confirmation from multiple independent sources significantly improves signal reliability and reduces false signals.
Types of Confirmation
Price confirmationA second bar confirming the direction of the signal bar. Reduces false positives at the cost of some entry slippage.
Volume confirmationVolume expanding in the direction of the signal. The single most important confirming indicator for chart patterns and breakouts.
Multiple indicator confirmationTwo or more independent indicators (trend + momentum + breadth) all pointing in the same direction.
Multiple timeframe confirmationSignal on the primary timeframe confirmed by the higher timeframe trend and the lower timeframe momentum.
Intermarket confirmationRelated markets confirming the directional signal (e.g., sector ETF confirming individual stock breakout).
Divergence (Negative Confirmation)

Divergence occurs when price and an indicator disagree. This is often a more powerful signal than confirmation.

Bearish divergence: Price makes a new high but indicator (RSI, MACD) fails to. Signals weakening momentum even as price rises.

Bullish divergence: Price makes a new low but indicator makes a higher low. Signals strengthening buying pressure beneath the surface.

Hidden divergence: indicator makes new extreme but price does not. Can confirm trend continuation.

Common Pitfalls

Over-confirmation: Waiting for too many confirmations results in late entries and reduced reward:risk.

Correlated indicators: Using two momentum oscillators (RSI + Stochastics) is not confirmation — they are derived from the same source data. True confirmation requires independent data sources.

Indicator selection: Mix trend-following (MA, MACD) with oscillators (RSI, Stochastics) for genuine independent confirmation.

Level I
Cycle Analysis
Markets move in recurring patterns driven by human behaviour and economic fundamentals. Cycle analysis identifies the dominant periodic patterns to project future turning points.
Economic Cycles
Kitchin Cycle~3–4 years. Business inventory cycle. Short-term fluctuations in inventory buildup and depletion.
Juglar Cycle~7–11 years. Business investment cycle. Capital expenditure and fixed investment fluctuations.
Kuznets Cycle~15–25 years. Infrastructure and construction cycle. Driven by demographics and real estate.
Kondratieff Wave~45–60 years. Long wave economic cycle identified by Nikolai Kondratieff. Driven by major technological innovations and debt supercycles.
Cycle Principles

Summation principle: All price activity is the result of the sum of all active cycles. The dominant cycle exerts the most influence.

Commonality: Similar cycles appear across different markets and time periods.

Variation: Cycles do not repeat with perfect periodicity — allow ±10–15% timing variance.

Nominality: Cycles tend to be longer during uptrends (right translation) and shorter during downtrends (left translation).

Seasonal Patterns
Presidential Cycle4-year US election cycle. Year 3 and pre-election year 4 tend to be strongest for equities historically.
"Sell in May"Historical weakness May–October vs. strength November–April. Not universally reliable but widely cited.
January EffectSmall-cap stocks historically outperform in January following tax-loss selling in December.
Monthly seasonalityOptions expiration effects, window dressing, and quarterly rebalancing create recurring intramonth patterns.
Level I
Market Strength & Breadth
Breadth indicators measure the internal health of a market move by analysing the participation of individual components, not just the index level.
Key Breadth Indicators
Advance-Decline LineRunning total of (advancing issues − declining issues) each day. Should trend in same direction as price index. Divergence = warning.
Advance-Decline RatioAdvances / Declines. Oscillator version of A-D data. Used with smoothing to identify extremes.
McClellan OscillatorDifference between 19-day and 39-day EMAs of net advances. Short-term overbought/oversold breadth indicator.
McClellan Summation IndexCumulative version of McClellan Oscillator. Longer-term breadth trend indicator.
High-Low Index52-week new highs vs. new lows. Above 50 = more new highs (bullish). Below 50 = more new lows (bearish).
% Above Moving AveragePercentage of index components trading above their 50 or 200 SMA. Above 80% = overbought. Below 20% = oversold.
Arms Index (TRIN)Trading Index = (Advances/Declines) / (Advancing Volume/Declining Volume). Above 1 = bearish bias; Below 1 = bullish bias. Contrarian at extremes.
Key CMT Concept: Breadth Divergence
When an index makes a new high but the A-D line fails to confirm, it signals that fewer and fewer stocks are driving the rally. This internal deterioration often precedes a significant correction by weeks or months. The 1999–2000 market top was preceded by significant breadth divergence.
Level I
Sentiment Analysis
Sentiment indicators measure the psychological state of market participants. At extremes, they function as contrarian signals — the crowd is most bullish at tops and most bearish at bottoms.
Survey-Based Sentiment
AAII SurveyWeekly survey of retail investors. Extreme bullishness (>55%) = contrarian bearish signal. Used as a contrary indicator.
Investors IntelligenceSurveys investment newsletter writers. Bull ratio vs. bear ratio. Extreme bullish consensus historically precedes corrections.
NAAIM Exposure IndexActive money manager equity exposure. Extreme high = caution; extreme low = opportunity.
Market-Based Sentiment
Put/Call RatioTotal put volume / call volume. High ratio = excessive fear (contrarian bullish). Low ratio = complacency (contrarian bearish).
VIX (Fear Index)Implied volatility of SPX options. Above 30 = fear; below 15 = complacency. Spikes near market lows, troughs near tops.
Short Interest RatioTotal short interest / average daily volume. High short interest = significant short squeeze potential if price rallies.
COT ReportCommitments of Traders. Commercial hedgers ("smart money") vs. large speculators. Extremes in commercial net position are contrarian signals.
Margin DebtHigh NYSE margin debt historically peaks near market tops as investor leverage reaches maximum. Decline from peak is concerning.
Level I
EMH, Random Walk & Behavioural Finance (L1)
Understanding the academic critique of technical analysis and the behavioural finance arguments that support it.
Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
Weak FormAll past prices and volume are already reflected in current prices. Technical analysis cannot consistently generate alpha. Historical data has no predictive power.
Semi-Strong FormAll publicly available information is immediately reflected in prices. Neither technical nor fundamental analysis can consistently beat the market.
Strong FormAll information, including insider information, is reflected in prices. No one can consistently beat the market. Generally rejected empirically.
Behavioural Finance Arguments for TA
AnchoringInvestors anchor to prior prices (e.g., purchase price), creating predictable zones of decision-making — the basis for S/R levels.
Loss AversionLosses feel ~2× more painful than equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman-Tversky). Creates reluctance to sell losers, supporting downtrend continuation.
Herd BehaviourFollowing the crowd creates momentum. Trends persist because participants copy each other. Technical trend-following exploits this.
OverconfidenceTraders overestimate their skill and the precision of signals. Creates excessive trading and contributes to momentum.
Recency BiasOver-weighting recent events. Drives trend continuation — recent winners are expected to keep winning.
Disposition EffectTendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long. Creates predictable supply at prior highs (resistance).
Noise Trader RiskNoise traders (uninformed) can move prices persistently away from fundamental value. Technical analysts can identify and follow these moves.
Level I
Basic Statistics for Technical Analysts
Statistical concepts underpin objective evaluation of TA systems. Without statistics, backtesting results are meaningless.
Descriptive Statistics
Mean (μ) = ΣX / N Variance (σ²) = Σ(X − μ)² / N Std Dev (σ) = √Variance Skewness = E[(X−μ)³] / σ³ Kurtosis = E[(X−μ)⁴] / σ⁴ − 3

In returns distributions: positive skew = more small losses with occasional large gains. Negative skew (fat left tail) = more common small gains but catastrophic losses. Leptokurtosis (fat tails) is common in financial returns — normal distribution assumptions underestimate tail risk.

Probability Concepts

Expected Value: The probability-weighted average outcome.

E = (Win% × Avg Win) − (Loss% × Avg Loss)

A positive expectancy system is the goal of all system design.

Bayes' Theorem — updating probabilities with new evidence. Relevant when combining multiple TA signals.

Standard Error — the standard deviation of the sampling distribution. Important for assessing backtesting result reliability.

Hypothesis Testing for Trading Systems
Null Hypothesis (H₀)The system has no edge — any positive results are due to randomness/data mining.
Significance Level (α)Threshold for rejecting H₀. Commonly 5% (p < 0.05) or 1% (p < 0.01). Lower α = more stringent test.
Type I ErrorFalse positive — rejecting H₀ when it is true. Concluding a system has edge when it doesn't. Dangerous in trading.
Type II ErrorFalse negative — failing to reject H₀ when it is false. Missing a real edge.
Multiple Testing ProblemTesting many parameters/rules increases the chance of finding spurious patterns that don't hold out-of-sample.
Level II
Elliott Wave Theory
Developed by Ralph Nelson Elliott in the 1930s. Markets move in fractal wave patterns driven by collective crowd psychology. Fibonacci ratios connect the waves.
The 5-3 Wave Structure
Markets move in an impulse of 5 waves in the direction of the larger trend (waves 1, 2, 3, 4, 5) followed by a corrective 3-wave structure in the opposite direction (waves A, B, C). This pattern repeats at every degree of trend — the fractal principle.
Wave Rules (Inviolable)
Rule 1
Wave 2 CANNOT retrace more than 100% of Wave 1. If it does, the wave count is invalid.
Rule 2
Wave 3 can NEVER be the shortest of Waves 1, 3, and 5. It is typically the longest.
Rule 3
Wave 4 CANNOT overlap the price territory of Wave 1 (except in diagonals and leveraged instruments).

Wave 5 extensionOften equals Wave 1 in length. Can extend to 1.618× Wave 1 if Wave 3 was extended.
Alternation principleIf Wave 2 is simple (zigzag), Wave 4 tends to be complex (flat or triangle), and vice versa.

Level II
Oscillators & Momentum Indicators
Bounded indicators measuring momentum and rate of change. Most effective in ranging markets but also useful for divergence signals in trending markets.
RSI Interactive Demo
RSI Period 14
RSI = 100 − [100 / (1 + RS)] RS = Average Gain (N periods) / Average Loss (N periods) Standard period = 14. Overbought > 70, Oversold < 30.
MACD
Moving Average Convergence Divergence. Gerald Appel's trend-following momentum indicator.
MACD Line = EMA(12) − EMA(26) Signal Line = EMA(9) of MACD Line Histogram = MACD Line − Signal Line

Signal line crossover: MACD crosses above signal = bullish. Below = bearish.

Zero line crossover: MACD crosses above/below zero. Confirms trend change.

Histogram divergence: Histogram shrinking before a signal line crossover — early warning.

Stochastics
Developed by George Lane. Measures where the close is relative to the high-low range over N periods.
%K = (Close − Lowest Low(N)) / (Highest High(N) − Lowest Low(N)) × 100 %D = 3-period SMA of %K (signal line)

Overbought > 80, Oversold < 20. Crossover of %K above %D in oversold zone = buy signal. Divergence from price is the strongest signal.

Slow stochastic: %D becomes the new %K; smoothed further. Reduces false signals.

Other Key Oscillators
Williams %RInverted stochastic. Range -100 to 0. Below -80 = oversold; above -20 = overbought.
Rate of Change (ROC)(Close − Close(N)) / Close(N) × 100. Measures percentage change over N periods. Unbounded momentum oscillator.
CCI (Commodity Channel)Measures deviation from average price. Above +100 = overbought; below -100 = oversold. Also used as trend indicator above/below zero.
ATRAverage True Range. Not an oscillator but a volatility measure. True Range = Max(H-L, |H-PC|, |L-PC|). Used for stops and position sizing.
Oscillator Divergence Types
Regular BearishPrice: higher high. Indicator: lower high. Trend weakening. Most common reversal warning.
Regular BullishPrice: lower low. Indicator: higher low. Selling pressure diminishing. Potential reversal.
Hidden BearishPrice: lower high. Indicator: higher high. Trend continuation downward.
Hidden BullishPrice: higher low. Indicator: lower low. Trend continuation upward. Pullback entry signal.
Level II
Advanced Indicators
More sophisticated tools that combine multiple data sources or measure specific market characteristics such as trend strength, money flow, and market structure.
ADX — Average Directional Index
Developed by J. Welles Wilder. Measures the strength of a trend, not its direction. Used with +DI and -DI for directional bias.
TR = Max(H-L, |H-PC|, |L-PC|) +DM = H − PH (if positive, else 0) -DM = PL − L (if positive, else 0) +DI = 100 × EMA(+DM) / ATR -DI = 100 × EMA(-DM) / ATR ADX = 100 × EMA(|+DI − -DI| / (+DI + -DI))

ADX > 25 = trending market. ADX < 20 = ranging market. ADX rising = trend strengthening. ADX falling = trend weakening regardless of price direction.

Ichimoku Cloud
Developed by Goichi Hosoda. An all-in-one indicator providing support/resistance, trend direction, and momentum.
Tenkan-sen = (9H + 9L) / 2 (Conversion line) Kijun-sen = (26H + 26L) / 2 (Base line) Senkou Span A = (Tenkan + Kijun) / 2 (leading, 26 fwd) Senkou Span B = (52H + 52L) / 2 (leading, 26 fwd) Chikou Span = Close plotted 26 periods back

Price above cloud = bullish. Price below cloud = bearish. A thick green cloud = strong support. Thin cloud = weak support.

Parabolic SAR
Wilder's Parabolic Stop and Reverse. Provides trailing stops that accelerate as a trend develops.
SAR(t) = SAR(t-1) + α × (EP − SAR(t-1)) α (acceleration factor) = 0.02 start, max 0.20 EP = Extreme Point (highest high or lowest low)
Price above SAR = long. Price crosses below SAR = reverse to short. Best used in trending markets; generates excessive signals in ranging conditions.
Keltner Channels

Similar to Bollinger Bands but uses ATR instead of standard deviation for band width. Less sensitive to single large moves.

Middle = EMA(20) Upper = EMA(20) + 2 × ATR(10) Lower = EMA(20) − 2 × ATR(10)

When Bollinger Bands are inside Keltner Channels = squeeze signal. Breakout of both channels = very strong move.

Level II
Intermarket Analysis
John Murphy's framework for understanding the relationships between bonds, stocks, commodities, and currencies. These relationships provide leading signals and context for TA.
Core Intermarket Relationships
Bonds ↔ StocksTypically inversely related. Rising yields (falling bond prices) increase the discount rate and compress equity valuations. Relationship can break down in deflationary crises (both fall).
Bonds ↔ CommoditiesInverse. Rising commodity prices signal inflation, pushing bond yields higher (prices lower). Commodity prices lead the inflation cycle.
USD ↔ CommoditiesInverse. Dollar-denominated commodities become more expensive globally when USD strengthens, reducing demand and prices.
USD ↔ GoldStrongly inverse. Gold is priced in dollars and is an inflation/uncertainty hedge. Dollar weakness = gold strength.
USD ↔ EquitiesMixed. A strong dollar can pressure multinational earnings (negative for large-caps). A strong dollar also attracts capital inflows to US equities.
Oil ↔ Energy SectorStrong positive correlation. Oil prices lead energy sector stocks and have secondary effects on the broader market through inflation.
Business Cycle Sector Rotation (Stovall / Merrill Lynch)
Early ExpansionFinancials, Consumer Discretionary, Technology. Credit expanding; consumers spending; growth expectations rising.
Mid ExpansionIndustrials, Materials, Technology. Capital expenditure cycle accelerates; infrastructure investment.
Late ExpansionEnergy, Materials, Real Estate, Staples. Inflation rising; commodity prices peak; yield curve flattening.
Contraction / RecessionHealthcare, Utilities, Consumer Staples. Defensive sectors. Earnings resilience in downturn; dividend yield attractive vs. falling rates.
Early RecoveryFinancials (again), Consumer Discretionary. Rate cuts begin; credit conditions ease.
Level II
Volatility Analysis & Options (VIX)
Volatility is a critical dimension of market analysis. It is mean-reverting, cyclical, and provides insight into market fear and complacency. Essential for options-aware technical analysts.
VIX — CBOE Volatility Index
The VIX measures the 30-day implied volatility of SPX options. It represents the market's expectation of future realised volatility, expressed as an annualised percentage.
VIX < 15Low volatility / complacency. Markets calm. Often near market peaks when used as a contrarian indicator.
VIX 15–25Normal/moderate volatility range. No strong contrarian signal.
VIX > 30Elevated fear. Often near market lows. Spikes above 40 historically signal capitulation and near-term reversal opportunity.
VIX Term StructureContango (VIX spot < VIX futures) = normal. Backwardation (spot > futures) = acute fear. Shift from contango to backwardation is an early warning for long SPX positions.
VIX/VXV RatioShort-term vs. 3-month implied volatility. Ratio > 1 = near-term fear spike; below 1 = normal. Mean-reverts quickly.
Historical vs. Implied Volatility

Historical Volatility (HV): Realised volatility calculated from actual price changes over a lookback period. Backward-looking.

HV = StdDev(Daily Returns, N) × √252

Implied Volatility (IV): The volatility embedded in option prices. Forward-looking. Reflects the market's consensus expectation.

IV > HV = options are expensive. IV < HV = options are cheap. Premium sellers prefer high IV; premium buyers prefer low IV.

Volatility for TA Practitioners

ATR position sizing: Risk = N × ATR. Ensures consistent dollar risk across instruments and market regimes.

Bollinger Band squeeze: Narrowing bands signal historically low volatility — a volatility expansion breakout is imminent.

Low VIX + extreme breadth: Combination of complacency and overextended breadth is a high-confidence warning for medium-term correction risk.

0DTE/weekly options: High gamma exposure creates amplified price moves at key S/R levels as dealers hedge — creates self-fulfilling technical reactions.

Level II
System Design & Testing
Objective, rule-based trading systems remove emotional bias and allow rigorous performance evaluation. Evidence-based TA requires statistical validation of signals and strategies.
System Components
Entry rulesObjective conditions that trigger a position. Must be unambiguous and testable historically.
Exit rulesStop loss (risk management) + profit target or trailing stop (trend following). Equal importance to entry.
Position sizingHow much capital to risk per trade. The most important driver of long-term performance outcomes.
Universe filterWhich instruments to trade and when. Can dramatically affect results.
Backtesting Pitfalls

Data mining bias: Testing many rules on the same data will find patterns that don't hold out-of-sample.

Overfitting: Too many parameters curve-fit to historical noise. Use minimum parameters.

Survivorship bias: Testing only on currently listed stocks ignores companies that went bankrupt.

Look-ahead bias: Using data that wasn't available at the time of the signal.

Transaction costs: Slippage, spreads, and commissions must be realistically modelled.

Key Performance Metrics
Profit Factor = Gross Profit / Gross Loss (want > 1.5) Expected Value = (Win% × Avg Win) − (Loss% × Avg Loss) Sharpe Ratio = (Return − Risk-free Rate) / StdDev(Returns) Sortino Ratio = (Return − Risk-free Rate) / Downside Deviation Calmar Ratio = CAGR / Maximum Drawdown Max Drawdown = (Peak − Trough) / Peak × 100
Robustness Testing

Walk-forward analysis: Optimise on in-sample period, test on subsequent out-of-sample period. Repeat rolling forward.

Monte Carlo simulation: Randomise trade order to assess the range of possible equity curves. Identifies worst-case drawdown scenarios.

Parameter sensitivity: Test a range of parameter values. Robust systems perform well across a range, not just at one optimised value.

Ned Davis: Being Right vs. Making Money

A system with 40% win rate can be highly profitable if average winners are significantly larger than average losers. Win rate alone is meaningless without considering the payoff ratio.

Minimum Win Rate = 1 / (1 + Avg Win / Avg Loss) Example: 2:1 R:R → needs only 33.3% win rate to break even
Level II
Crypto & Digital Assets
Technical analysis is particularly well-suited to crypto markets — they are sentiment-driven, trade 24/7, and lack fundamental anchors that would override technical signals.
Why TA Applies to Crypto

Crypto markets exhibit all the conditions that make TA effective: trending behaviour, recognisable chart patterns, volume-confirming moves, Fibonacci retracements, and strong sentiment-driven cycles. The absence of fundamental earnings anchors means price is driven almost entirely by supply/demand dynamics and sentiment.

The 24/7 trading cycle means daily candlesticks represent a complete session but without the opening gap dynamics of equity markets.

Crypto-Specific Indicators
Stock-to-Flow (S2F)Ratio of existing supply to new annual production. Bitcoin's halving cycle creates predictable supply shocks modelled by S2F.
MVRV RatioMarket Value / Realised Value. Compares current market cap to the average cost basis of all coins. High MVRV = historically overbought.
NVT RatioNetwork Value / Transaction Volume. Crypto's equivalent of P/E. High NVT = overvalued vs. utility.
Hash RateMining difficulty. Rising hash rate = network security increasing = bullish long-term signal for Bitcoin.
Funding RatePerpetual futures funding. Positive = longs paying shorts (bullish crowding). Extreme positive = contrarian bearish.
Halving Cycle Framework

Bitcoin's block reward halves approximately every 4 years (every ~210,000 blocks). This supply shock has historically been followed by a bull cycle of 12–18 months.

The four-year halving cycle maps onto a rough pattern: accumulation pre-halving → bull market → euphoria top → bear market → next halving.

Technical analysts apply standard charting tools — trend lines, Fibonacci extensions, volume profile — to identify entry and exit points within this cycle framework.

The Asymmetry of Losses
A 10% loss requires an 11.1% gain to recover. A 25% loss requires a 33.3% gain. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain. A 75% loss requires a 300% gain. This asymmetry is why risk management is the primary concern at Level III.
Stop Placement Methods
StructuralBelow key swing low (long) or above swing high (short). Most logical invalidation point for the trade thesis.
ATR-basedEntry ± N × ATR. Adapts to current volatility regime. Typically 1.5–2.5× ATR for swing trades.
TrendlineBelow the trendline that justified the entry. Violated trendline = trade thesis invalidated.
Fixed % (fallback only)Simple but ignores market structure. Use only as a maximum loss limit, not as primary stop.
Chandelier ExitTrailing stop = Highest High (N) − N × ATR. Allows trend trades to breathe while protecting gains.
Kelly Criterion & Position Sizing
Kelly % = W − [(1−W) / R] W = Win rate R = Average Win / Average Loss (reward ratio) Example: 50% win rate, 2:1 R:R Kelly = 0.5 − (0.5/2) = 25% Recommended: Use Half-Kelly (12.5%) for safety

Full Kelly maximises long-run growth but results in extreme drawdowns. Half-Kelly (or fractional Kelly) balances growth with survivability. Most professional traders risk 0.5–2% per trade regardless of Kelly calculation.

Level III
Portfolio Management with TA
Integrating technical analysis into portfolio construction, position management, and client communication for professional asset management contexts.
TA in Portfolio Context

Asset allocation: Intermarket analysis and trend signals determine tactical over/underweight across asset classes relative to a strategic benchmark.

Security selection: Relative strength analysis identifies the strongest securities within a sector or universe for overweight positions.

Timing: TA provides entry and exit signals for building or reducing positions. Scaling in/out at technical levels improves average execution price.

Risk monitoring: Ongoing technical monitoring of positions allows early exit before stops are hit in deteriorating markets.

Portfolio-Level Risk

Correlation risk: Positions that appear diversified by sector may have high price correlation during stress events. Monitor correlation matrices.

Beta management: Portfolio beta can be managed using index derivatives when individual position liquidation is impractical.

Drawdown limits: Define maximum portfolio drawdown levels that trigger defensive action (reduce risk, hedge, or liquidate).

Sector concentration: Breadth of trend across sectors — if only one or two sectors are driving portfolio performance, risk is concentrated.

Investment Policy Statement (IPS) Considerations
Return objectiveDefine absolute or relative return targets. TA-based systems should have a clearly defined expected return based on backtesting and forward validation.
Risk toleranceMaximum drawdown, maximum position size, stop-loss protocols. These must be documented and adhered to.
Liquidity needsAffects position sizing and instrument selection. Illiquid positions require wider stops and smaller sizes.
Time horizonDetermines which timeframe of TA is appropriate — scalping strategies are inappropriate for a pension fund.
Regulatory constraintsSome clients have restrictions on derivatives, leverage, short positions, or specific asset classes.
Level III
Relative Strength Analysis
Robert Levy's research showed that stocks with the strongest 26-week relative performance tend to continue outperforming. Relative strength is a core tool for security selection and sector rotation.
Types of Relative Strength
Price ratio (RS Ratio)Security price / Benchmark price. Rising ratio = outperformance. Chart the ratio and apply standard TA to it.
Levy RS RankRank securities by (current price / 26-week average price). Top quartile = buy candidates; bottom quartile = sell.
Sector RSSector ETF / Index ratio identifies leadership and rotation. Use RS analysis to position in leading sectors.
RS Ratio = Asset Price / Benchmark Price Normalised RS = (RS / RS Moving Average − 1) × 100
RRG — Relative Rotation Graphs

Developed by Julius de Kempenaer. Plots JdK RS-Ratio (x-axis) vs. JdK RS-Momentum (y-axis) for multiple securities simultaneously. Each security traces a path through four quadrants as its relative strength evolves.

Leading (top right)Strong RS and improving momentum. Current outperformers. Overweight.
Weakening (bottom right)Strong RS but fading momentum. Prior leaders rotating out. Begin reducing.
Lagging (bottom left)Weak RS and declining momentum. Current underperformers. Underweight.
Improving (top left)Weak RS but strengthening momentum. Future leaders emerging. Begin accumulating.
Trading Rule
Sectors rotate clockwise through the four RRG quadrants. Entering positions in the "Improving" quadrant before the move to "Leading" provides the best risk/reward entry points in sector rotation trades.
Level III
Asset Relationships
Understanding how different asset classes interact in different market regimes is essential for portfolio-level technical analysis and risk management.
Correlation Analysis
Correlation is not static. Relationships that hold in normal markets can break down or reverse during stress periods. The analyst must monitor rolling correlations.
Stable positive (0.7–1.0)Assets move together reliably. Diversification benefit is limited. Risk is concentrated.
Unstable / regime-dependentStocks/bonds positive in deflationary crises (both fall); negative in inflationary cycles. Requires regime-aware analysis.
Crisis correlationIn liquidity crises, correlations spike toward +1 as investors sell everything to raise cash. Diversification fails at the worst time.
Yield Curve & Equities

Steep curve: Long rates well above short rates. Banks profit from carry; credit expands; bullish for growth assets.

Flat curve: Convergence signals slowing growth expectations. Neutral for equities.

Inverted curve: Short rates above long rates. Historically precedes recession by 6–18 months. Bearish for cyclical equities; bullish for defensive sectors.

Technical analysts chart the 2s10s spread (10-year minus 2-year Treasury yield) as a key intermarket indicator.

Gold as a Signal Asset

Gold/Silver ratio: Rising ratio = risk aversion (gold outperforms silver). Falling ratio = risk appetite. A classic risk-on/risk-off indicator.

Gold/SPX ratio: Rising = gold outperforming; defensive environment. Falling = equities outperforming; risk-on.

Real yields: Gold is most sensitive to real (inflation-adjusted) yields. Falling real yields = bullish for gold regardless of nominal rate direction.

Level III
Behavioural Finance (Level III)
At Level III, behavioural finance is applied to explain market anomalies, justify TA, and inform client communication. Understanding why patterns exist makes them more credible and persistent.
Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky)
People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point (usually purchase price), not in absolute terms. Losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good.
This asymmetry explains resistance levels at prior highs (holders who bought at the top are eager to break even and sell) and support at prior lows (buyers who sold with losses re-enter when price returns).
Market Anomalies & TA
Momentum EffectStocks that performed well in the past 3–12 months tend to continue outperforming. The foundation of trend-following. Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993.
Mean ReversionExtreme performers tend to revert toward the mean over longer horizons (3–5 years). Supports oscillator-based contrarian signals.
January EffectSmall-caps outperform in January following year-end tax-loss selling. Predictable seasonal anomaly.
Post-Earnings DriftStocks drift in the direction of earnings surprises for weeks after announcement. Technical trend-followers can capture this.
Applying Behavioural Finance to Clients

Setting loss limits in advance: Use TA stop levels to pre-commit to exit rules before emotions become involved in the decision.

Systematic rebalancing: Forces selling of recent winners and buying of recent laggards — countering recency bias.

Communicating risk: Showing clients a historical drawdown chart of the strategy manages expectations and reduces panic-driven redemptions.

Rules-based process: A documented, objective trading system removes the analyst's discretionary decisions from the equation — important for fiduciary credibility.

Level III
Classical Methods Integration Review
Level III requires integration of classical TA tools — applying them together in a coherent analytical framework rather than in isolation.
The Technical Analysis Process
Step 1: Macro / IntermarketIdentify the macroeconomic regime. What is the trend in USD, bonds, commodities? What business cycle phase are we in?
Step 2: Index / Market AnalysisWhat is the primary trend of the major indices? Is breadth confirming? What does volume tell us about trend health?
Step 3: Sector AnalysisWhich sectors are showing relative strength? Use RRG or RS ratios to identify leaders and laggards.
Step 4: Individual SecurityWithin the leading sectors, identify securities at technical entry points (breakouts, pullbacks to MA, pattern completions).
Step 5: Entry TimingUse shorter-term charts to time the entry. Confirm with volume and oscillator signals.
Step 6: Position ManagementPlace stop based on structure. Define profit target using measured move. Monitor ongoing trend health.
Multiple Timeframe Framework

Higher timeframe (Monthly/Weekly): Identifies primary trend direction. Trade only in this direction unless specific reversal criteria are met.

Middle timeframe (Daily): Identifies the intermediate swing and potential entry pattern (breakout, pullback, pattern completion).

Lower timeframe (4h/1h/5min): Times the entry precisely. Momentum oscillators and volume confirm the micro-level entry signal.

Conflicting Signals

When different indicators or timeframes conflict, the analyst should:

1. Default to the higher timeframe trend. Lower timeframe signals against the primary trend have lower probability.

2. Require additional confirmation before acting against the trend.

3. Reduce position size when conviction is lower due to conflicting signals.

4. Use the conflict itself as information — a market that can't make new highs despite bullish indicators is signalling distribution.

Level III
Performance Measurement
Objective performance measurement allows systematic evaluation of strategy effectiveness and comparison across different approaches and risk levels.
Performance Metrics Reference
Annualised Return (CAGR) = (End Value / Start Value)^(1/Years) − 1 Annualised Volatility = StdDev(Daily Returns) × √252 Sharpe Ratio = (CAGR − Risk-free Rate) / Annualised Volatility Sortino Ratio = (CAGR − Risk-free Rate) / Downside Deviation Calmar Ratio = CAGR / |Maximum Drawdown| Information Ratio = Active Return / Tracking Error Max Drawdown = (Peak Equity − Trough Equity) / Peak Equity × 100 Profit Factor = Total Gross Profits / Total Gross Losses Expectancy = (Win% × Avg Win) − (Loss% × Avg Loss) Recovery Factor = Net Profit / Maximum Drawdown
Risk-Adjusted Return Comparison

Sharpe Ratio penalises all volatility equally (both upside and downside). A Sharpe above 1.0 is considered good; above 2.0 is exceptional.

Sortino Ratio only penalises downside volatility. Better measure for trend-following systems that have asymmetric returns.

Calmar Ratio focuses on the worst-case drawdown scenario. Preferred by traders who experience large drawdowns.

Information Ratio measures active return per unit of tracking error. Relevant when benchmarked against an index.

Trade-Level Metrics
Win Rate% of trades that are profitable. Meaningless in isolation — must be paired with payoff ratio.
Average RAverage multiple of initial risk returned per trade. 1.0R = break even; 2.0R = 2× initial risk returned.
Largest Losing TradeMonitors for catastrophic single-trade losses that indicate stop discipline failure.
Consecutive LossesThe maximum string of losing trades. Determines minimum account size needed to withstand drawdown.
Average Hold TimeDetermines the timeframe the system operates in and annualisation assumptions.
